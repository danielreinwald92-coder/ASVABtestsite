Lesson 1

What is Arithmetic Reasoning?

Let's start with the basics. Arithmetic Reasoning is just word problems. You'll read a short story, figure out what math to do, and solve it.

Here's the good news: you only need basic math — adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing. The tricky part is figuring out WHICH operation to use.

The secret to word problems:

Read → Translate → Solve → Check

Let's break that down:

The 4-Step Method

1 Read the problem carefully. What are they asking for?
2 Translate the words into math (we'll teach you how!)
3 Solve the math problem
4 Check — does your answer make sense?
💡 Pro tip: Always read the LAST sentence first! That's usually what they're asking you to find.

Quick Check

In Arithmetic Reasoning, what's the hardest part?

  • A Doing the math calculations
  • B Reading fast enough
  • C Figuring out which math operation to use
  • D Memorizing formulas

Lesson 2

Translating Words to Math

Here's where the magic happens. Certain words ALWAYS mean certain math operations. Once you know these, word problems become way easier.

Words are just math in disguise

Addition Words (+)

When you see these words, you'll probably ADD:

sum, total, combined, altogether, increased by, more than, plus, in all

→ "John has 5 apples and gets 3 more" = 5 + 3

Subtraction Words (−)

When you see these words, you'll probably SUBTRACT:

difference, fewer, less than, decreased by, remain, left over, minus

→ "She had 10 cookies and ate 4. How many remain?" = 10 − 4

Multiplication Words (×)

When you see these words, you'll probably MULTIPLY:

times, product, of, each, per (sometimes), doubled, tripled

→ "5 boxes with 12 items each" = 5 × 12

Division Words (÷)

When you see these words, you'll probably DIVIDE:

per, each (when splitting), divided by, ratio, split equally, average

→ "60 miles in 3 hours. What's the speed per hour?" = 60 ÷ 3
⚠️ Watch out: "Less than" is backwards! "5 less than 12" means 12 − 5, not 5 − 12.

Try It

"Maria earned $240 and split it equally among 4 friends." What operation?

  • A Addition
  • B Division
  • C Multiplication
  • D Subtraction

Lesson 3

Ratios: Comparing Things

Ratios are the #1 most tested topic on Arithmetic Reasoning. Good news — they're actually pretty simple once you get the hang of it.

A ratio just compares two numbers. "3 to 4" or "3:4" or "3/4" all mean the same thing.

The Golden Rule of Ratios

Set up a proportion: a/b = c/d Then cross-multiply!

Real Example

Problem: If 3 apples cost $2, how much do 12 apples cost?

1 Set up the proportion: 3 apples / $2 = 12 apples / $?
2 Write it as fractions: 3/2 = 12/x
3 Cross-multiply: 3 × x = 2 × 12
4 Solve: 3x = 24, so x = 8
✓ Answer: $8
💡 Always check your work: 3 apples = $2 means each apple is about 67 cents. 12 × $0.67 = $8. ✓ It matches!

Your Turn

A car travels 150 miles on 6 gallons of gas. How many gallons are needed to travel 400 miles?

  • A 14 gallons
  • B 15 gallons
  • C 16 gallons
  • D 18 gallons

Lesson 4

Percentages Made Simple

Percentages show up everywhere — discounts, taxes, tips, test scores. Let's make them easy.

"Percent" literally means "per 100." So 25% just means 25 out of 100, or 25/100, or 0.25.

The Percent Formula

Part = Whole × Percent (as decimal)

Converting Percents to Decimals

Just move the decimal point 2 places LEFT:

→ 25% = 0.25
→ 8% = 0.08
→ 150% = 1.50

Finding a Percent of Something

What is 20% of 80?

1 Convert: 20% = 0.20
2 Multiply: 80 × 0.20 = 16

Discounts (Percent OFF)

A $50 jacket is 20% off. What's the sale price?

1 Find the discount: $50 × 0.20 = $10 off
2 Subtract: $50 − $10 = $40

Shortcut: 20% off means you pay 80%. So: $50 × 0.80 = $40

⚠️ Common trap: If something INCREASED by 20% to become $60, don't just subtract 20%! The original was $60 ÷ 1.20 = $50.

Your Turn

A $80 jacket is 25% off, then you pay 8% sales tax. What's the final price?

  • A $60.00
  • B $64.80
  • C $68.40
  • D $72.00

Lesson 5

Distance, Rate, and Time

These problems are everywhere on the ASVAB. A car travels, a plane flies, someone walks... they all use the same simple formula.

The DRT Triangle

Distance = Rate × Time D = R × T   |   R = D ÷ T   |   T = D ÷ R

Basic Example

A car travels at 60 mph for 3 hours. How far does it go?

→ Distance = Rate × Time = 60 × 3 = 180 miles

Finding Rate (Speed)

You drove 150 miles in 3 hours. What was your speed?

→ Rate = Distance ÷ Time = 150 ÷ 3 = 50 mph
Two Objects Moving

• Moving TOWARD each other? ADD their speeds.

• Moving AWAY from each other? ADD their speeds.

• Moving in the SAME direction? SUBTRACT their speeds.

Two Cars Problem

Two cars start together, going opposite directions at 55 mph and 45 mph. When are they 300 miles apart?

1 They're moving apart, so ADD speeds: 55 + 45 = 100 mph
2 Time = Distance ÷ Rate = 300 ÷ 100 = 3 hours
⚠️ Never average speeds! If you drive 30 mph one way and 60 mph back, the average speed is NOT 45 mph. You need to calculate total distance ÷ total time.

Final Challenge

A plane flies 600 miles in 2 hours. How long will it take to fly 1,500 miles at the same speed?

  • A 4 hours
  • B 5 hours
  • C 6 hours
  • D 7.5 hours

The Secret to Vocabulary

Here's something most people don't know: you don't need to memorize thousands of words. Instead, learn about 50 word parts, and you can figure out the meaning of thousands of words.

English words are like LEGO blocks. They're built from smaller pieces:

Words = Prefix + Root + Suffix

UN
not
+
BELIEV
believe
+
ABLE
capable of
=
UNBELIEVABLE
not capable of being believed

See how that works? If you know the pieces, you can decode any word — even ones you've never seen before!

💡 On the ASVAB: You only have 30 seconds per question. There's no time to think hard. Knowing word parts lets you make fast, confident guesses.

Lesson 2

Prefixes: The Beginning

Prefixes go at the START of words and change the meaning. Here are the most important ones:

UN-, IN-, IM-, DIS- = NOT

These all mean "not" or "opposite of":

→ unhappy = NOT happy
→ invisible = NOT visible
→ impossible = NOT possible
→ disagree = NOT agree

RE- = AGAIN

→ redo = do AGAIN
→ rebuild = build AGAIN
→ review = view AGAIN

PRE- = BEFORE

→ preview = view BEFORE
→ preheat = heat BEFORE
→ predict = say BEFORE (it happens)

More Key Prefixes

→ anti- = against (antiwar, antibody)
→ sub- = under (submarine, subway)
→ super- = above (superhero, supernatural)
→ mis- = wrong (mistake, misunderstand)

Try It

What does "prehistoric" most likely mean?

  • A After history
  • B Against history
  • C Before history
  • D Under history

Lesson 3

Roots: The Core Meaning

Roots are the heart of the word. They carry the main meaning. Learn these and you unlock huge chunks of English vocabulary.

BENE- = GOOD

→ benefit = something GOOD
→ benevolent = wishing GOOD (kind)
→ benefactor = one who does GOOD

MAL- = BAD

→ malice = BAD intent
→ malfunction = work BADLY
→ malevolent = wishing BAD (evil)
Notice the pattern?
BENE (good) + volent = kind
MAL (bad) + volent = evil

Same ending, opposite meanings — because the ROOT changed!

More Key Roots

→ aud- = hear (audio, audience, audible)
→ vid/vis- = see (video, vision, visible)
→ dict- = say/speak (dictate, predict, dictionary)
→ port- = carry (transport, portable, export)
→ rupt- = break (rupture, interrupt, erupt)

Try It

BENEVOLENT most nearly means:

  • A Angry
  • B Kind
  • C Loud
  • D Weak

Lesson 4

Test-Taking Strategy

You only have 30 seconds per question. Here's how to use that time wisely:

The 3-Step Speed Method

Step 1: Positive or Negative?

First, decide: is this word GOOD or BAD?

→ "Benevolent" sounds like "bene" = good. It's POSITIVE.
→ "Hostile" sounds harsh. It's NEGATIVE.

Now eliminate answers with the wrong tone!

Step 2: Look for Word Parts

Break the word down. Do you recognize any pieces?

→ "Inaudible" = in (not) + aud (hear) + ible (able) = not able to be heard

Step 3: Trust Your Gut

If you've heard the word before, your first instinct is usually right. Don't overthink!

⚠️ Common Trap: Watch for words that SOUND similar but mean different things.

• "Illicit" (illegal) vs "Elicit" (draw out)
• "Accept" (receive) vs "Except" (exclude)

Final Challenge

IMPEDE most nearly means:

  • A Help
  • B Speed up
  • C Block
  • D Move

Lesson 1

What is Math Knowledge?

Good news: Math Knowledge is NOT word problems. It's straightforward math — you see an equation, you solve it.

Bad news: You only have 75 seconds per question and no calculator. So you need to know your stuff!

What You Need to Know

Algebra + Geometry + Basic Number Rules

The Good News

It's all stuff you learned in school:

→ Solving equations (find x)
→ Exponents (x², x³)
→ Area and perimeter
→ Basic geometry (triangles, circles)
💡 Pro tip: Since there's no calculator, practice doing math by hand. And memorize common squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100.

Quick Check

What makes Math Knowledge different from Arithmetic Reasoning?

  • A You can use a calculator
  • B No word problems — just straight math
  • C It's only about geometry
  • D You have unlimited time

Lesson 2

Solving Equations

Equations are like a balance scale. Whatever you do to one side, you MUST do to the other.

The Golden Rule

Keep the equation balanced!

Example: Solve 3x + 5 = 20

1 We want x alone. First, get rid of the +5
2 Subtract 5 from BOTH sides: 3x = 15
3 Now get rid of the 3. Divide BOTH sides by 3: x = 5
✓ Check: 3(5) + 5 = 15 + 5 = 20 ✓
The order matters!
1. First, add or subtract to move numbers
2. Then, multiply or divide to get x alone

Example: Solve 5x - 12 = 3x + 8

1 Get all x's on one side. Subtract 3x from both: 2x - 12 = 8
2 Add 12 to both sides: 2x = 20
3 Divide both sides by 2: x = 10

Your Turn

Solve: 4x + 7 = 31

  • A x = 4
  • B x = 5
  • C x = 6
  • D x = 8

Lesson 3

Exponents Made Easy

Exponents are just shorthand for repeated multiplication. x³ means x × x × x.

The 3 Exponent Rules

Rule 1: Multiplying = ADD exponents

→ x³ × x⁴ = x⁷ (because 3 + 4 = 7)

Think of it: (x·x·x) × (x·x·x·x) = x·x·x·x·x·x·x = x⁷

Rule 2: Dividing = SUBTRACT exponents

→ x⁸ ÷ x³ = x⁵ (because 8 - 3 = 5)

Rule 3: Power of a power = MULTIPLY exponents

→ (x³)² = x⁶ (because 3 × 2 = 6)

Think of it: (x³)² = x³ × x³ = x⁶

Special cases:
• x⁰ = 1 (anything to the zero power equals 1)
• x¹ = x (anything to the first power is itself)

Your Turn

Simplify: (x²)³ × x⁴

  • A x⁹
  • B x⁸
  • C x¹⁰
  • D x²⁴

Lesson 4

The Pythagorean Theorem

This is THE most tested geometry concept. It works for right triangles (triangles with a 90° angle).

a² + b² = c²

c is always the LONGEST side (hypotenuse)

Example: Find the missing side

A right triangle has legs of 3 and 4. What's the hypotenuse?

1 a² + b² = c²
2 3² + 4² = c²
3 9 + 16 = c²
4 25 = c², so c = 5
🔥 Memorize these "Pythagorean Triples":

• 3 - 4 - 5 (most common!)
• 5 - 12 - 13
• 8 - 15 - 17

Multiples work too! 3-4-5 doubled = 6-8-10

Your Turn

A right triangle has legs of 6 and 8. What's the hypotenuse?

  • A 9
  • B 10
  • C 12
  • D 14

Lesson 5

Circles: Area and Circumference

You WILL see circle problems. Here are the two formulas you need:

Circle Formulas

Area = πr²   |   Circumference = 2πr
⚠️ #1 Mistake: Using diameter instead of radius!

Radius = distance from center to edge
Diameter = distance across the whole circle = 2 × radius

If they give you diameter, divide by 2 first!

Example: Circle with diameter 10

Find the area.

1 Diameter = 10, so radius = 10 ÷ 2 = 5
2 Area = πr² = π × 5² = π × 25 = 25π
💡 On the ASVAB: Answers are often left in terms of π (like "25π" instead of "78.54"). Don't waste time calculating π = 3.14...

Final Challenge

A circle has radius 6. What is its area?

  • A 36π
  • B 12π
  • C 6π
  • D 72π

Lesson 1

Reading Smarter, Not Harder

Paragraph Comprehension isn't about reading fast — it's about reading smart. Here's the key insight:

The answer is ALWAYS in the passage

Don't guess. Don't use outside knowledge. Find it in the text.

This is actually great news! You don't need to know anything about the topic. You just need to find where the answer is hiding.

The Smart Reading Method

1 Read the QUESTION first (not the passage!)
2 Now you know what to look for
3 Read the passage with that question in mind
4 Find the sentence that answers it
5 Pick the answer that matches
💡 Pro tip: You have about 2.5 minutes per question — that's plenty of time to re-read parts of the passage. Don't rush!

Quick Check

What should you do FIRST when tackling a Paragraph Comprehension question?

  • A Read the question
  • B Read the whole passage carefully
  • C Look at the answer choices
  • D Guess based on the topic

Lesson 2

Finding the Main Idea

"What is this passage mainly about?" — You'll see this question a lot. Here's how to nail it every time.

Main Idea = The BIG Picture

Not a tiny detail. Not too broad. Just right.

Where to Find It

1 Check the FIRST sentence — it often states the main point
2 Check the LAST sentence — it often summarizes
3 Ask yourself: "If I had to summarize this in ONE sentence, what would I say?"
Eliminate wrong answers:

❌ Too narrow — Only covers one detail from the passage
❌ Too broad — Goes beyond what the passage discusses
✓ Just right — Covers the whole passage, nothing more

Example Passage

"The honeybee is one of the most important insects on Earth. Without bees, many foods we eat would not exist. Bees pollinate about 80% of flowering plants, including many fruits and vegetables."

Main idea? Bees are important because they pollinate our food.

NOT "bees pollinate 80% of plants" (too specific) or "insects are important" (too broad)

Try It

A passage about how smartphones have changed communication would have which main idea?

  • A Phones can send text messages
  • B Smartphones have transformed how people communicate
  • C Technology is changing the world
  • D The first iPhone came out in 2007

Lesson 3

Making Inferences

"It can be inferred..." or "The author implies..." — These questions ask you to read between the lines.

Inference ≠ Guessing

It's a logical conclusion SUPPORTED by the text

How Inferences Work

"Sarah grabbed her umbrella and raincoat before leaving the house."

What can we infer?

✓ It was raining or about to rain (supported by umbrella + raincoat)
✗ Sarah is afraid of getting wet (not supported — we don't know her feelings)
✗ It was winter (not supported — rain happens in any season)
⚠️ Common trap: An answer might be TRUE in real life, but if it's not supported by THIS passage, it's WRONG.
💡 The test: Can you point to words in the passage that support your inference? If not, pick a different answer.

Try It

"Fleming discovered penicillin in 1928 but couldn't produce it in large quantities. Mass production methods weren't developed until 1941."

What can be inferred about penicillin before 1941?

  • A It was widely used in hospitals
  • B It was only available in limited amounts
  • C Fleming kept it secret
  • D It didn't work very well

Lesson 4

Elimination Tricks

Even if you're not 100% sure of the right answer, you can often eliminate the wrong ones.

Words That Signal Wrong Answers

Extreme Words = Usually WRONG

Be suspicious of answers containing:

❌ always, never, all, none, every, only

These are too absolute. Real passages rarely make such extreme claims.

Moderate Words = Often RIGHT

These are safer bets:

✓ some, many, often, may, might, usually, sometimes

These leave room for exceptions, which is more realistic.

Quick Elimination Checklist:

❌ Does it use extreme words? (Probably wrong)
❌ Is it too narrow? (Just one detail)
❌ Is it too broad? (Beyond the passage)
❌ Does it contradict the passage?
❌ Is it not mentioned at all?
💡 When stuck between two answers: Go back to the passage and find the EXACT sentence that supports one of them. The right answer almost always has direct textual support.

Final Challenge

Which answer is MOST LIKELY to be correct for a reading question?

  • A "All scientists agree that..."
  • B "This never happens because..."
  • C "This often occurs when..."
  • D "Everyone always believes..."
